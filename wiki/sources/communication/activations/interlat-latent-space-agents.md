# Enabling Agents to Communicate Entirely in Latent Space (Interlat)

## One-liner

![[interlat-latent-space-agents/one-liner]]

## Summary

**Interlat** replaces all inter-agent communication with **last-layer hidden state sequences** — the sender's autoregressive hidden states are transmitted directly to the receiver via a lightweight communication adapter. Unlike single-vector approaches ([[activation-communication-harvard|AC]]), Interlat transmits the **full temporally-aligned sequence** of hidden states from all generation steps. Unlike [[thought-communication-multiagent|ThoughtComm]], it requires no autoencoder — raw hidden states are transmitted with only a small adapter for rescaling.

## Core Innovation: Bandwidth

A discrete token carries ~15 bits. A hidden state carries ~40,000 bits — a **2,600x bandwidth increase** per position. This enables transmitting the sender's full reasoning trajectory, including uncertainty and alternative paths that sampling collapses.

> [!notation|right]
> | Component | Notation |
> |---|---|
> | Hidden states | $H \in \R^{L \times d}$ |
> | Boundary tokens | `⟨bop⟩` / `⟨eop⟩` |

- **Sender (reasoning model)** generates reasoning via standard autoregressive decoding; last-layer hidden states collected at each step form matrix $H \in \R^{L \times d}$. For each decoding step $\tau = 1 \ldots L$: $h_\tau = \text{Transformer}(x_{m+\tau-1})_{m+\tau-1}$, collecting into $H = [h_1, h_2, \ldots, h_L]$.
- **Communication adapter**: Multi-head self-attention (8 heads) followed by layer normalization, then an adaptive projection module. This rescales and transforms hidden states from the sender's representation space into a form the receiver can interpret. The adapter is lightweight and trainable.
- **Receiver (actor model)** processes input embeddings as: $E = [e(x_1), \ldots, e(x_i), h_1, \ldots, h_L, e(x_j), \ldots]$ where $x_i$ = `<bop>` (begin of plan) and $x_j$ = `<eop>` (end of plan) are special boundary tokens.

### Training Losses (Full Detail)

The total objective is: $\Loss_\text{total} = \Loss_\text{task} + \lambda_S \cdot \Loss_\text{sep} + \lambda_A \cdot \Loss_\text{align}$ where $\lambda_S, \lambda_A > 0$.

**1. $\Loss_\text{task}$ (Cross-entropy utility)**:

Standard cross-entropy on correct output tokens at supervised positions $S$:

> $$\Loss_\text{task} = -\frac{1}{|S|} \sum_{t \in S} \log p(Y_t \mid C_t, H)$$

Ensures the model produces accurate, coherent responses. This is weighted at 1.0.

**2. $\Loss_\text{sep}$ (Conditional thought separation)**:

Weighted Jensen-Shannon Divergence between matched latents $H$ and mismatched latents $\tilde{H}$ (sampled from a **different task within the same batch**):

> $$\Loss_\text{sep} = -\frac{1}{|S|} \sum_{t \in S} \text{JSD}\!\left( p(\cdot \mid C_t, H),\; p(\cdot \mid C_t, \tilde{H}) \right)$$

This forces the receiver to actually distinguish task-specific latent content from irrelevant latents. Without it, the model shortcuts by ignoring latent input entirely. Negative samples come from different tasks within the same batch (harder discrimination target than random noise). Dynamic coefficient: $\lambda_\text{sep}$ annealed from 0.1 to 1.0 (7B/8B models) or 0.1 to 0.5 (0.5B model).

**3. $\Loss_\text{align}$ (Plan-aligned regulation)**:

Combines KL divergence and cosine similarity between latent-conditioned predictions and text-conditioned predictions (using the CoT plan $P$ generated by the same instruction-tuned model):

> $$\Loss_\text{align} = \frac{1}{|S|} \sum_{t \in S} \text{KL}\!\left( p(\cdot \mid H) \;\|\; p_\text{plan}(\cdot \mid P) \right) + \frac{1}{|S|} \sum_{t \in S} \left(1 - \cos(z(H),\; z_\text{plan}(P))\right)$$

where $z$ and $z_\text{plan}$ are the corresponding normalized logits. This prevents the separation objective from being exploited — without it, the model shifts probability mass toward idiosyncratic tokens that increase JSD divergence without helping the task. Dynamic coefficient: $\lambda_\text{align}$ annealed from 0.1 to 0.2 for all models.

### Curriculum Learning (Full Detail)

Learning to interpret latents from scratch is unstable. The **stochastic token-to-latent mixing** curriculum works as follows:

- A replacement rate $r$ is sampled uniformly from $\{0, 0.1, 0.2, \ldots, 1.0\}$ per batch
- The first $r \cdot L$ positions use **token embeddings** from the corresponding CoT plan
- The remaining positions use actual **latent hidden states**
- The mixed communication is: $H^{(r)} = [e_1, \ldots, e_{r \cdot L},\; h_{r \cdot L+1}, \ldots, h_L]$

This creates a smooth transition from fully text-based communication (r=1.0) to fully latent communication (r=0). Without curriculum, success drops from 70.48% to 33.10% (seen) and from 65.42% to 20.65% (unseen), with highly unstable training dynamics.

### Training Setup

- 8 NVIDIA A100-80G GPUs, global batch size 16 (2 per GPU)
- AdamW optimizer, learning rate 1e-5, 3% warm-up ratio
- Mixed precision (bfloat16), FlashAttention-2, DeepSpeed ZeRO-2 with CPU offload
- 5% validation split for model selection with batch-level early stopping
- Base models (Qwen2.5-7B-Base, 0.5B-Base, LLaMA3.1-8B-Base) used as actors to isolate latent communication effects from instruction-tuning priors
- Latents generated from corresponding instruction-tuned models (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct, 0.5B-Instruct, LLaMA3.1-8B-Instruct)

## Information Compression

### Compression via Latent-Space Reasoning

Full-length latents $H_L \in \R^{L \times d}$ are highly expressive but $L$ can be hundreds of steps, causing communication latency. The compression mechanism trains a separate **reasoning model $M$** to produce compact latents $H_K \in \R^{K \times d}$ where $K \ll L$.

The key mechanism is an **autoregressive latent-space reasoning loop** — the reasoning model feeds its last hidden state back as the next input embedding through a lightweight projection ("bridge module"):

> $$M(E_i) \to h_i, \quad E_{i+1} = E_i \;\|\; \text{Proj}(h_i)$$

This loop runs $K$ times, generating $K$ latent vectors entirely in latent space without ever decoding to tokens. The actor model and its communication adapter are **frozen** during compression training — only the reasoning model parameters are updated.

### Compression Training Objective

$\Loss_\text{compress} = \lambda_\text{task} \cdot \Loss_\text{task} + \lambda_\text{pref} \cdot \Loss_\text{pref} + \lambda_\text{geom} \cdot \Loss_\text{geom}$ (all weights = 1.0 in practice)

Three paths through the frozen actor:
- **Path A** (compressed latents $H_K$): the generated compressed message
- **Path D** (full-length latents $H_L$): reference from instruction-tuned model
- **Path B** (no latents): baseline with no communication

$\Loss_\text{task}$: Cross-entropy on frozen actor's predictions conditioned on $H_K$ — ensures compressed latents are useful.

$\Loss_\text{pref}$ **(Uncertainty-weighted agreement)**: Per-token weights $w_t = \max(H(p_t^B) - H(p_t^D), 0)$ emphasize positions where full latents actually reduce predictive uncertainty. The agreement loss is:

> $$\Loss_\text{pref} = \frac{T^2}{\sum w_t} \sum_{t \in S} w_t \cdot \text{KL}(p_t^D \| p_t^A)$$

This teaches $H_K$ to reproduce the informative behavioral effects of $H_L$ while ignoring positions where latents are unhelpful.

$\Loss_\text{geom}$ **(Latent direction alignment)**: Aligns global direction of actor-side latent features. Step-averaged directions $\bar{z}^A$ and $\bar{z}^D$ are computed, then:

> $$\Loss_\text{geom} = 1 - \cos(\bar{z}^A, \bar{z}^D)$$

This preserves geometric consistency between compressed and uncompressed latent spaces. Empirically the most critical compression loss.

Compression training: 64 A100-80GB GPUs, AdamW with learning rate 5e-5, warmup ratio 3%.

CoT wins on easier problems (linguistic constraints act as beneficial regularizer, pruning search space). **Interlat surpasses CoT on Level-5** — the hardest problems — because forced text discretization causes "premature collapse" of the reasoning distribution, while Interlat maintains a superposition of parallel hypotheses in continuous space.

### Cross-Family Results

**Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (sender) -> LLaMA3.1-8B-Base (actor)**: 70.95% seen / 71.39% unseen. This is the **best result in the entire paper**, beating even same-family Interlat. Since Qwen and LLaMA have distinct latent manifolds, the improvement cannot be attributed to superficial architectural compatibility — it suggests genuine latent-level inter-agent understanding that transfers across heterogeneous representations. Aligns with findings that heterogeneous LLM agents outperform homogeneous ensembles due to complementary inductive biases.

### The "Aha Moment": Training Dynamics

The separation loss ($\Loss_\text{sep}$) reveals a distinctive learning curve:
- **Steps 0-2000**: $\Loss_\text{sep}$ plateaus near $\ln(2) = 0.69$, indicating zero effective distinction between matched and mismatched latent messages
- **~Step 2200**: Sharp drop in $\Loss_\text{sep}$ — the model suddenly begins distinguishing task-relevant from irrelevant latents
- This marks the moment the actor learns to exploit latent information rather than ignoring it

This pattern resembles phase transitions in learning and supports the interpretation that latent comprehension emerges abruptly rather than gradually.

## Limitations

- **Single-environment evaluation for multi-agent**: The multi-agent experiments are evaluated exclusively on ALFWorld, a text-based household task environment. Whether Interlat's latent communication advantages generalize to other multi-agent domains (e.g., open-ended dialogue, game-playing, collaborative coding) remains unvalidated.
- **Small model scale**: Experiments are limited to 0.5B-8B parameter models (Qwen2.5-0.5B, Qwen2.5-7B, LLaMA3.1-8B). Scaling behavior to frontier-size models (70B+) is unknown — the adapter and curriculum may require different tuning at larger scales.
- **No cross-family evaluation in multi-agent setting**: The cross-family result (Qwen2.5-7B → LLaMA3.1-8B) is tested only in the single sender-receiver pair configuration. Whether cross-family latent communication works in full multi-agent topologies with more than two agents is unexplored.
- **Curriculum sensitivity**: Without the stochastic token-to-latent mixing curriculum, performance drops catastrophically (70.48% → 33.10% seen, 65.42% → 20.65% unseen). This makes the training procedure fragile — the annealing schedule and replacement rate distribution are critical hyperparameters that may need re-tuning for different model families or task domains.
- **Requires white-box access**: Both sender and receiver internals must be accessible for hidden state extraction and injection, making Interlat incompatible with black-box API-served models.

## Connections

- **[[activation-communication]]**: Interlat is the full-sequence extension of AC's single-vector approach. AC replaces one activation at one layer; Interlat transmits temporally-aligned sequences of last-layer hidden states.
- **[[latentmas-collaboration|LatentMAS]]**: Both transmit hidden states across agents, but LatentMAS uses KV-cache transfer (training-free); Interlat uses raw hidden states with a trained adapter (more flexible, works cross-family).
- **[[embedding-space-communication]]**: Interlat operates at the deepest level — last-layer hidden states rather than output embeddings. The 2,600x bandwidth advantage over tokens is the most extreme data point for the [[continuous-vs-discrete-representation]] argument.
- **[[latent-space-reasoning]]**: The compression mechanism is itself a form of latent-space reasoning — the reasoning model performs autoregressive generation entirely in latent space via the bridge module, connecting to [[coconut-reasoning-latent-space|Coconut]]'s approach of feeding hidden states back as inputs.
- **[[catastrophic-forgetting]]**: Interlat deliberately uses base models as actors to avoid leaking instruction-tuning priors, sidestepping the forgetting barrier entirely.

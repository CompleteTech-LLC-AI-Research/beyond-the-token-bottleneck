# Compression & Information-Theoretic Bounds

How much bandwidth is *enough* for latent reasoning and communication? Every system in this wiki transmits continuous representations between models or across reasoning steps, but no two systems agree on how much information is needed. Interlat sends 2,600x more bits per position than text and then compresses 46x with only 4% loss. SoftCoT matches 24 discrete tokens with 6 continuous ones. [[kvcomm-kth-selective|KVComm]] discovers that 30% of layers carry nearly all the useful signal. LatentCompress squeezes an entire multi-agent message into 512 bytes.

These results converge on a striking claim: **the vast majority of what models transmit is redundant for the task at hand.** The question is whether there exists a principled lower bound --- a minimum bandwidth below which communication quality degrades --- and how that bound varies with task complexity, model scale, and communication depth.

This MOC traces a reading path from the theoretical foundations of the continuous-vs-discrete gap, through the empirical compression results that quantify redundancy, to the "selective beats full" paradox, and finally to the open questions that define the frontier.

---

## Guided Reading Path

### 1. Why Continuous Is Richer Than Discrete

Start with the theoretical foundation. A single token carries ~15 bits ($\log_2 V$ for $V = 32{,}000$). A single hidden state carries ~40,000 bits. But the gap is not just quantitative --- discrete sampling destroys uncertainty (the model's confidence distribution), superposition (multiple simultaneous hypotheses), and nuance (fine-grained distinctions the tail of the distribution encodes). The information lost at the discrete bottleneck is precisely the information that enables BFS-like reasoning in [[coconut-reasoning-latent-space|Coconut]] and richer debate in CIPHER.

**[[continuous-vs-discrete-representation]]** --- The unifying theoretical page. Covers the information-theoretic gap, the Expected SARSA analogy, the quantum superposition formalization (Zhu et al., NeurIPS 2025), and the depth bottleneck ([[cot-expressivity-theory|Feng et al.]], NeurIPS 2023) showing that the discrete-to-continuous shift is a complexity-class transition, not merely an efficiency gain.

---

### 2. The 2,600x Bandwidth Experiment --- and 46x Compression

With the theoretical motivation in place, Interlat provides the most extreme empirical data point. Full hidden-state sequences give a 2,600x bandwidth increase over text, and the system beats discrete CoT on the hardest problems (Level-5 MATH) because continuous space preserves the superposition of reasoning paths that sampling collapses. But the compression results are equally important: trained compression to just 8 latent steps retains 94--96% of accuracy with a 46x speedup, demonstrating that even within continuous space, massive redundancy exists.

**[[interlat-latent-space-agents]]** --- Interlat: full hidden-state communication, cross-family transfer (Qwen-to-LLaMA achieves the paper's best result), and the detailed compression table showing graceful degradation from full-length to 8-step latents.

---

### 3. Soft Tokens Are 4x More Efficient Than Hard Tokens

SoftCoT provides a complementary compression data point from the reasoning side. Six continuous "soft thought tokens" match the performance of 24 discrete tokens generated by the same assistant model --- a ~4x compression ratio consistent with CCoT's reported 5x. Critically, this ratio holds even when the assistant is tiny (0.5B), suggesting that the compression advantage is a property of the continuous medium itself, not of model capability.

**[[softcot-efficient-reasoning]]** --- SoftCoT: externalized latent reasoning via a small frozen assistant. The 4x compression ratio, the [[catastrophic-forgetting|catastrophic forgetting]] finding (Coconut damages instruction-tuned models), and the result that assistant model size barely matters (0.5B $\approx$ 7B).

---

### 4. Extreme Compression: 512 Bytes and the Task-Dependent Bandwidth Floor

LatentCompress pushes compression to its current extreme. A slot-attention architecture (4 slots x 64 dimensions = 512 bytes) matches single-agent baseline accuracy on GSM8K while replacing MB-scale [[kv-cache-communication|KV-cache transfers]] --- roughly a 1/2000 compression ratio. But the results are sharply task-dependent: GPQA-Diamond drops from 26.8% (full KV) back to 19.1% at 512 bytes. The key variable is not the compression method but the *intrinsic bandwidth requirement of the task*. Simple arithmetic needs minimal context; complex multi-hop scientific reasoning cannot be summarized in 512 bytes.

The project also establishes a cumulative degradation model ($Q \propto e^{-T\varepsilon/C}$) for multi-agent chains, quantifying how compression error compounds across agents.

**[[latentcompress-open-call]]** --- LatentCompress: 512-byte compression, task-dependent bandwidth floors, the information bottleneck + adversarial training result (4x compression with style leakage control), and three proposed research directions including native latent communication pretraining.

---

### 5. The "Selective > Full" Paradox

KVComm introduces a result that reframes the compression question entirely. Rather than compressing *within* a representation, it selects *which layers* to share --- and discovers that 30% of layers' KV pairs match or approach full-context performance. More surprisingly, selective sharing sometimes **exceeds** the skyline (full context concatenation). This means that full communication is not just redundant --- it can be actively harmful, introducing noise that selective sharing filters out.

The layer selection strategy reveals a principled structure: intermediate layers (semantic abstractions) are most transferable, while early layers (surface patterns) and late layers (task-specific predictions) add noise. A single calibration sample is sufficient to identify the optimal layer set, and the selection generalizes across all inputs.

**[[kvcomm-kth-selective]]** --- KVComm: attention-based layer selection, the selective-beats-full finding, non-contiguous layer selection, and the systematic evidence that hidden states are a poor communication medium (information concentration bias in late layers).

---

### 6. Open Questions and Theoretical Gaps

The empirical results above raise questions that no existing paper answers. What is the theoretical minimum bandwidth for lossless inter-agent communication? Is there an optimal compression point between full continuous and full discrete that maximizes the information-compatibility trade-off? When does discrete actually win --- are there tasks where discretization's structure serves as beneficial regularization (as hinted by Interlat's CoT advantage on easier MATH problems)?

**[[open-questions]]** --- All open questions aggregated across the wiki. The "Information Theory & Capacity Bounds" and "Cross-Architecture Compatibility" sections are most relevant here --- particularly the question of whether the continuous-discrete gap widens or narrows as models scale.

---

### 7. Frontier Directions: Toward Principled Compression

The frontier research directions synthesize these threads into actionable research programs. Direction #8 (Learned Compression of Latent Communication) directly addresses the central question of this MOC: nobody has established information-theoretic bounds on minimum communication bandwidth, designed rate-distortion optimal latent codecs, or tested how the minimum varies with task complexity and model scale. Direction #3 (The Self-Improvement Effect) is also relevant --- KVComm's selective-beats-full result may be an instance of a broader phenomenon where latent-space mediation acts as beneficial regularization.

**[[frontier-research-directions]]** --- Eight paradigm-shift research directions. Direction #8 (learned compression) and Direction #3 (self-improvement effect) connect most directly to compression bounds.

Taken together, these sources suggest a layered structure to the compression question:

This points toward an adaptive compression framework: systems that dynamically allocate bandwidth based on task complexity, transmitting 512 bytes for arithmetic and megabytes for scientific reasoning.
